# Format-Invariant SFT: Closing the Absorption-Integration Gap in Continual Knowledge Injection

## Abstract

Continual knowledge-injection methods for large language models are typically evaluated on in-format absorption probes that silently conflate parameter storage with behavioral generalization. The gap between parameter storage and behavioral availability under prompt-format shift is well-documented in adjacent literatures: the Reversal Curse (Berglund et al. 2023), the storage-vs-extraction distinction (Allen-Zhu & Li 2023, 2024), and editing ripple-effect failures (Cohen et al. 2024; Zhong et al. 2023). This paper *measures* that gap quantitatively in the specific setting of continual LoRA-based knowledge injection on a task-tuned 4B model and tests one objective-level intervention against it. We first show, across six LoRA-based update procedures --- three novel variants of Continual-Optimization Policy Ranking (COPR, Zhang et al. 2025), plus naive SFT, KL-regularized SFT, and a continual-SFT baseline --- that no objective modification in the COPR family beats simple KL-regularized SFT on absorption at 4B/LoRA scale. At 3K batch edits, KL-regularized SFT leads the COPR family by 36-40% in absorption F1 at 10-20x less compute; across 15 sequential rounds of 200 disjoint edits, KL-regularized SFT leads per-round absorption 9-3 (2 ties). A behavioral QD-format probe on the same round-15 checkpoints reproduces the absorption-integration gap (Berglund 2023; Allen-Zhu & Li 2023/2024) in our LoRA continual-editing setup: every method absorbs the injected fact in the QA probe format more strongly than it transfers to the downstream task format (format gap 0.05-0.14 F1 across methods), with gold-injection variants exhibiting the *widest* behavioral gap despite the *smallest* geometric hidden-state shift ratio --- a specific inversion pattern we have not seen previously reported, though the broader principle that geometric/parametric metrics overstate behavioral success is documented (Wan et al. 2025; Wei et al. 2024; Hernandez et al. 2023). Motivated by this diagnosis, we apply Risk Extrapolation (V-REx; Krueger et al. 2021) to prompt formats, treating each format as a V-REx "environment" and each per-format cross-entropy as the environment risk. The resulting training procedure --- which we call **Format-Invariant SFT (FI-SFT)** --- optimizes mean-cross-entropy across K prompt formats of the same fact plus an explicit variance penalty `mu * Var_k [log pi_theta(y_i | F_k(x_i))]` that punishes cross-format log-prob divergence. The loss is mathematically identical to V-REx; the mathematical novelty of this paper is therefore zero. What *is* novel is the application (continual knowledge injection in 4B LoRA-tuned LLMs, not image OOD classification), the empirical discovery that mixed-format SFT without a variance penalty *hurts* format transfer for factual knowledge (the knowledge-injection analog of a task-level finding by UIT, 2023), and the demonstration that the V-REx variance term is the minimal-viable fix. Trained for 15 chained rounds at matched compute against KL-regularized SFT, FI-SFT delivers: +19% absorption F1 (probe-level, 0.140 vs 0.118), +18% absorption fact-mean F1 (0.158 vs 0.134), +16% absorption fact-worst F1 (0.120 vs 0.103), +25% locality overall F1 (0.060 vs 0.048), +29% behavioral QD-format F1 (0.092 vs 0.072), and a format gap cut nearly in half (0.037 vs 0.072). Preservation Recall@10 trades off by 0.84 SE (0.213 vs 0.240). A three-way matched-compute ablation confirms that the variance penalty is doing non-trivial work: at one-round matched compute, plain mixed-format SFT is strictly worse than the single-format baseline (format gap 0.106 vs 0.082) while FI-SFT recovers to 0.073. The paper's contribution is therefore twofold: an empirical diagnosis of the absorption-integration gap under LoRA fine-tuning using both geometric and behavioral probes, and a novel method --- derived by flipping COPR's conceptual structure and adding an explicit cross-format consistency penalty --- that closes a meaningful portion of that gap. Numbers are quoted verbatim from Phase 1-8 CSV artifacts; standard errors and probe counts are reported with the means.

## 1. Introduction

Injecting new factual knowledge into the parameters of an already-tuned language model is an operational need in any deployment where the world changes after training. Existing methods fall into two broad families: constrained parameter edits that localize changes to a small set of MLP weights (ROME, Meng et al. 2022; MEMIT, Meng et al. 2023), and fine-tuning-style updates that use gradient descent on edit-specific objectives (SFT, KL-regularized SFT, direct-preference-style objectives such as KDPO, Rafailov-style preference contrast extended to editing; COPR, Zhang et al. 2025). A third family --- retrieval-augmented generation --- side-steps parametric editing entirely and is therefore out of scope here.

Most of the editing literature reports results in one of two default postures: a single batch of edits applied once, or a stream of single edits applied one at a time with or without checkpoint chaining. These two postures correspond to meaningfully different operational settings. A batch update is a push: a data-engineering pipeline produces a list of new facts and a training loop internalizes them in a single pass. A sequential update is a drumbeat: disjoint batches of facts arrive on a schedule, each round inherits the previous round's checkpoint, and the model must absorb the new batch without catastrophic interference from prior rounds. The operational costs and the failure modes differ, and so does the appropriate inductive bias of the update method.

This paper starts from a failed hypothesis and ends with a method. We set out to test whether Continual-Optimization Policy Ranking (COPR, Zhang et al. 2025) --- a continual-alignment objective that anchors a policy to a reference via K-candidate MSE ranking --- would transfer to continual knowledge editing. It did not. At 4B/LoRA scale, no COPR variant we built (plain, gold-injected, anchored, or both) beat the simple KL-regularized SFT baseline on absorption in either the batch regime (1K/3K edits) or the sequential regime (15 rounds of 200 edits). Trying to make COPR work produced three useful byproducts: a diagnostic observation (the *K-sample-all-wrong pathology*), a clean negative result (anchoring without gold injection collapses absorption to F1 0.066, below the no-update baseline), and a mechanistic analysis (Phase 6 LoRA-delta subspaces + Phase 7 hidden-state geometry) that localized the failure mode to a universal *absorption-integration gap* under QA-formatted loss. These byproducts, together, pointed toward a different kind of objective --- one that flips COPR's ranking axis from answer-space to format-space. We call the resulting method **Format-Invariant SFT (FI-SFT)**.

Four contributions follow.

First, the paper documents a **regime-agnostic negative transfer of COPR** at 4B/LoRA. The full COPR family --- plain COPR, `copr_gold_injection` (novel; inject gold into the K-candidate set), `copr_gold_injection_anchored` (novel; + task-replay-normalized reference), and `copr_anchored` (novel; reference-anchoring without gold) --- runs at 10-20x KL-regularized SFT's compute and delivers absorption F1 equal or worse. Gold injection specifically, which earlier drafts framed as the core novel contribution, collapses the method toward KL-regularized SFT: once a correct candidate dominates the rank signal, the MSE fit approximates cross-entropy on the gold answer. `copr_anchored` alone is the cleanest negative: absorption F1 0.066, compositional token-F1 0.025, below the no-update baseline of 0.052. Earlier drafts reported a +21% `copr_gold_injection` absorption win at round 10; we extended the stream to round 15 and the win did not replicate (3 scattered wins across 14 contested rounds, not a trend). These results establish KL-regularized SFT as the correct baseline against which to measure any new method in this setting.

Second, the paper *measures* the absorption-integration gap in our specific setting using two complementary probes. The phenomenon itself --- parameter storage of a fact without behavioral availability under prompt-format shift --- is documented in the Reversal Curse (Berglund et al. 2023), the storage-vs-extraction distinction (Allen-Zhu & Li 2023, 2024), MQuAKE's "instead of integrating... rather hard coding" finding (Zhong et al. 2023), and RippleEdits (Cohen et al. 2024). What we add is its quantified measurement under continual LoRA injection on a task-tuned base, with a format-gap metric, plus an interpretive observation: among our six methods, gold-injection variants exhibit the *largest* behavioral gap (+0.12 to +0.14) despite the *smallest* geometric hidden-state shift ratio --- a specific inversion pattern that, to our knowledge, has not been previously reported, though the general principle that geometric/parametric metrics can overstate behavioral success is documented (Wan et al. 2025 "Mirage of Model Editing"; Wei et al. 2024 EVOKE; Hernandez et al. 2023 on linearity decoupling).

Third, the paper applies **Risk Extrapolation (V-REx; Krueger et al. 2021) to prompt formats in continual knowledge injection**, a combination we call Format-Invariant SFT (FI-SFT). V-REx was originally developed for OOD image classification; we port it unchanged to the LLM-SFT setting by treating each prompt format as an "environment" and each per-format cross-entropy as the environment risk. The mathematical novelty of the method is therefore zero; the application is novel in this domain, and the empirical findings below are the paper's contribution.

Fourth, the paper shows **V-REx-style variance penalization beats the strongest baseline (15-round KL-regularized SFT) at matched compute on every measured axis except preservation**. Trained for 15 chained sequential rounds against 15 rounds of KL-regularized SFT at matched compute, FI-SFT delivers: absorption F1 (probe-level) 0.140 vs 0.118 (+19%), fact-mean F1 0.158 vs 0.134 (+18%), fact-worst F1 0.120 vs 0.103 (+16%), locality overall F1 0.060 vs 0.048 (+25%), behavioral QD-format F1 0.092 vs 0.072 (+29%), and format gap 0.037 vs 0.072 (cut nearly in half). Preservation trades off by 0.84 SE (0.213 vs 0.240). A three-way matched-compute ablation (KL-regularized SFT vs plain mixed-format SFT vs FI-SFT, all at one round) confirms the variance penalty is doing non-trivial work: plain mixed-format SFT (without the penalty, i.e. PAFT-style diverse-prompt augmentation in the sense of Wei et al. 2025) is strictly *worse* than the single-format baseline on format gap (0.106 vs 0.082), while FI-SFT recovers to 0.073. The penalty, not the data diversity, is the active ingredient. UIT (2023) reported an analogous "mixed-format hurts" finding for task-format instruction tuning and repaired it via LLM-based format unification; PIT (Jiang et al. 2024, arXiv 2402.12847) reported a closer match in continued pre-training (~6 EM degradation from naive format mixing during knowledge acquisition, repaired via sequencing). We confirm the failure mode in factual knowledge injection via LoRA continual editing and repair it with an explicit variance-penalty loss term. The closest SFT-level cross-format consistency regularizer in the literature is LoRA-JS (Qiang et al. 2024, referenced in arXiv 2508.11383), which uses Jensen-Shannon divergence between output distributions of different prompt variants for general instruction-following; LoRA-JS reported a negative result. Whether the V-REx scalar-variance formulation specifically (rather than JS divergence, Group DRO's `max_k`, or IRM's invariance penalty) is necessary remains an open ablation we flag as priority follow-up.

The central finding is therefore: **adding K candidate answers to a ranking (COPR) does not help continual knowledge injection, but applying V-REx-style variance penalization across K prompt formats of the same fact does.** This reframes what the right design lever is in continual editing: not objective modifications on candidate answers, but explicit cross-format consistency constraints on the update loss (a known technique from OOD generalization, newly applied here). All compositional (two-hop) edits continue to degrade bridging-entity recall below the no-update baseline across every method tested here (including FI-SFT at 0.024 vs no-update 0.102) --- a shared limitation the paper reports prominently and takes as the next target for methodological work.

## 2. Related Work

**Parametric knowledge editing.** ROME (Meng et al. 2022) formulates single-fact editing as a rank-one update to a single MLP layer identified via causal tracing. MEMIT (Meng et al. 2023) extends this to mass-editing thousands of facts at once via a layer-wise least-squares formulation. MEND (Mitchell et al. 2022a) trains a hypernetwork that maps an edit-example gradient into a low-rank weight update, while SERAC (Mitchell et al. 2022b) keeps edits in an external memory with a scope classifier and counterfactual model. Knowledge-Neurons (Dai et al. 2022) identifies individual MLP neurons whose activations are causally tied to factual predictions. The Yao et al. (2023) survey taxonomizes these memory-based, meta-learning, and locate-then-edit families and explicitly flags continual and compositional evaluation as open gaps --- the two gaps the present paper's Phases 3-4 target. All five methods above are batch or single-shot editors; none is evaluated under a stream of disjoint edit rounds with checkpoint chaining, which is the sequential regime studied here.

**Preference-style editing.** KDPO (Rozner et al. 2024) is the closest prior work: it casts a single fact edit as a DPO (Rafailov et al. 2023) problem --- the new fact is the preferred completion and the old fact the rejected one --- with the pre-edit model as the KL reference. Two design points distinguish it from the COPR variants studied here. First, KDPO uses the pre-edit base as the KL anchor, which drifts as edits accumulate over rounds; COPR's KL-replay anchor (Section 3.2) regularizes instead against a task-replay mixture, which does not drift with the policy. Second, KDPO does not inject gold answers into a candidate pool --- it is a two-way DPO contrast, not a K-way ranking. Gold injection (this paper) addresses the K-sample-all-wrong failure mode that KDPO side-steps by construction but cannot exploit. IPO (Azar et al. 2024) and related work on DPO overfitting motivate the KL regularization terms in both `kl_reg_sft` and the COPR family.

**COPR and continual preference alignment.** COPR (Zhang et al. 2025, ACL Findings) was proposed for continual *preference* alignment: maintaining helpfulness/harmlessness across a stream of preference-labeled tasks via KL regularization anchored on a task-replay buffer, avoiding catastrophic alignment drift. CPPO (Zhang et al. 2024) is a parallel continual-RLHF line with replay. The present paper ports COPR's inductive bias from preference tasks to knowledge injection, treating each new fact as a mini preference task with the gold completion as the preferred candidate. The novel gold-injection modification (Section 3.3) is specifically required because, unlike in preference alignment where the preferred and rejected completions are both reasonable behaviors, in knowledge injection the pre-edit policy puts near-zero probability on the gold answer and self-samples will therefore all miss it.

**LoRA, PEFT, and LoRA-based editing.** Low-rank adaptation (Hu et al. 2022) is the training substrate used throughout; its rank-r structure implies a strong inductive bias toward a low-dimensional update subspace, which grounds the Phase 6 mechanistic analysis. Aghajanyan et al. (2021) give the empirical evidence that fine-tuning occupies an intrinsically low-dimensional subspace and justifies why rank 16-32 suffices for absorbing a fact. MELO (Yu et al. 2024) and LoRAHub (Huang et al. 2023) validate LoRA as a medium for editing and for cross-task composition respectively; the present paper stays within a single LoRA slot and lets the training objective arbitrate conflicts. Zhu et al. (2024) analyze asymmetries in LoRA B-vs-A subspaces, which motivates the first-principal-angle (input-subspace) metric used in Phase 6.

**Continual learning for LLMs.** InsCL (Wang et al. 2024) demonstrates that a similarity-selected task-replay buffer beats EWC-style regularization (Kirkpatrick et al. 2017) on instruction-tuning continual learning; Luo et al. (2023) empirically documents catastrophic forgetting under continual SFT, directly motivating the task-preservation axis (Recall@10 on qd_temporal_test) in Section 5. The Wu et al. (2024) continual-learning-for-LLMs survey places the methods here in the replay-plus-regularization quadrant of the design space. O-LoRA (Wang et al. 2023) proposes orthogonal subspace learning for continual tuning; the Phase 6 subspace-overlap analysis is partially a response to that line, and finds that the COPR variants do *not* occupy orthogonal subspaces --- they share a near-identical first principal direction.

**Compositional and multi-hop evaluation.** Cohen et al. (2024) shows that editing a single fact rarely propagates to its logical consequences (e.g., editing a spouse does not update "mother-in-law"); the RippleEdits benchmark is the direct motivation for Phase 4. MQuAKE (Zhong et al. 2023) is methodologically closest to the compositional probe set used here; the 500 two-hop probes (Section 4) are constructed on the same "edited-fact-as-bridge" pattern, adapted to financial triples, with second-hop templates inspired by HotpotQA (Yang et al. 2018), MuSiQue (Trivedi et al. 2022), and 2WikiMultihopQA (Ho et al. 2020). The degradation of bridging-entity recall across all updated methods (Section 5.3) is consistent with Cohen et al.'s ripple-effect finding.

**Knowledge-editing benchmarks.** Counterfact (Meng et al. 2022) and zsRE (Levy et al. 2017, re-used by De Cao et al. 2021) provide ground-truth edit tuples. This paper does not use these directly; it constructs its own fact-triple dataset from FNSPID financial news (Shah et al. 2023), which allows a fair temporal cutoff and a task-domain (query-decomposition) test set that the baseline is skilled at.

**Risk Extrapolation and cross-environment variance penalization.** V-REx (Krueger et al. 2021) introduced `R_VREx = Σ_e R_e(θ) + β * Var_e[R_e(θ)]` as a method for OOD generalization in image classification: treat training distributions as "environments" and penalize variance of per-environment risks, pushing the learner toward representations that generalize uniformly across environments. Our Section 3.4 ports this loss verbatim, treating each prompt format as a V-REx environment and each per-format cross-entropy as the environment risk. The paper does not claim the loss as novel; the contribution is the application and the empirical finding that the variance term is load-bearing in this domain. Group DRO (Sagawa et al. 2020) and invariant risk minimization (IRM, Arjovsky et al. 2019) are sibling methods from the same OOD family; we do not run them as baselines here but flag them in Section 7 as the natural next comparisons.

**Cross-view consistency regularization for LLMs.** R-Drop (Wu et al. 2021) penalizes symmetric KL between outputs under two dropout masks of the same input, and SimCSE (Gao et al. 2021) contrasts augmentations in sentence-embedding space. Both regularize against intra-model stochasticity for a fixed input, whereas FI-SFT regularizes across *different* inputs (reformatted prompts) with a shared target output. MixText (Chen et al. 2020) interpolates hidden states of differently-augmented examples for semi-supervised text classification --- also not mathematically equivalent to V-REx-on-formats. PAFT (Wei et al. 2025) is the closest prior work in the LoRA-SFT + format-robustness space: it samples diverse prompt formats per training example but adds no explicit regularizer, and reports format-robustness gains on multiple-choice benchmarks (HellaSwag std drops from 9.78 to 0.70). Our Phase 8b ablation shows that in continual knowledge injection, PAFT-style format diversity without a regularizer *hurts* format transfer at matched compute; the V-REx variance penalty is what flips the sign. UIT (2023) reports the same "format diversity hurts" failure mode for instruction-tuning and repairs it via an LLM-based format-unification preprocessing step, not a loss term. CoRE (2025) applies a variance penalty in MEMIT-style editing but over hidden-state value vectors --- a different substrate (representations, not losses) and a different method family (locate-then-edit, not gradient-based SFT). To our knowledge, no prior work applies V-REx-style `Var_k[CE_k]` variance penalization to continual knowledge injection via LoRA SFT.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 Problem formulation

Let f_theta be a task-tuned LM and let T = {(x_i, y_i)} be a set of new fact triples, each rendered as a question-answer pair (x_i, y_i). The knowledge-injection problem is to produce an updated model f_theta' that (i) **absorbs** the new facts --- i.e., generates y_i given x_i --- while (ii) **preserving** task capability on an in-domain test set, (iii) **localizing** the change so that unrelated facts are not disturbed, and (iv) **composing** with pre-existing knowledge for multi-hop reasoning. This paper instantiates the four objectives with four metric families:

- **Preservation**: Recall@10 of the task-tuned query-decomposition model on the held-out qd_temporal test split (n=104).
- **Absorption**: For each injected fact, exact-match, token-F1, and a paraphrase-worst-case F1 across k paraphrases of (x_i). Reported as fact-level means and worst-case F1.
- **Locality**: Token-F1 on three strata of unrelated facts --- same entity (n=182 probes), other sector (n=1817), same sector (n=1). The n=1 stratum is acknowledged and discussed in Limitations.
- **Compositional**: Two-hop QA probes (n=500, generated with Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite) measuring exact-match, final-answer containment, bridging-entity recall, and token-F1.

### 3.2 Base objective and methods

Let pi_theta denote the update policy (task-tuned backbone + update LoRA). Let pi_ref denote the pre-update reference. Let D_T be a mini-batch of fact QA pairs and D_R a task-replay mini-batch from the qd_temporal train split.

**naive_sft.** Standard cross-entropy on D_T only, no replay, no reference regularizer.

**kl_reg_sft.** Cross-entropy on D_T plus lambda * KL(pi_theta || pi_ref) on D_R, with lambda = 0.1. This is a strong generic baseline that pays for an extra replay forward pass but no candidate sampling.

**copr (paper-faithful).** For each (x_i, y_i) in D_T, sample K=8 candidate completions from the current pi_theta, score each against y_i with token-F1, rank, and fit pi_theta's per-candidate log-prob to the reference-anchored rank signal with an MSE loss. Add a gated gold-NLL SFT anchor (weight alpha=0.25, gate threshold 0.5, `always_apply=False`) and a KL replay loss on D_R with `replay_pct=0.05`. beta=0.1, partial_match_threshold=0.5, max_new_tokens=128.

**copr_gold_injection (novel).** As above, except the gold completion y_i is injected as one of the K candidates (replacing the lowest-ranked self-sample). SFT anchor is OFF (alpha=0.0). This is the core novel contribution: it guarantees a correct anchor in the ranking even when self-samples are uniformly wrong, which is typical when the fact is genuinely unknown to pi_theta.

**copr_gold_injection_anchored (novel).** Gold injection ON plus the gated gold-NLL SFT anchor (alpha=0.25). This tests whether the two ideas compose or interfere.

**copr_anchored (novel, negative result).** Paper-faithful COPR, plus a task-replay-normalized reference policy: log pi_ref'(y|x) = log pi_ref(y|x) + task_anchor_alpha * logmeanexp_{y_j ~ D_R}(log pi_ref(y_j|x_j)), with task_anchor_alpha=0.3 and task_anchor_n_samples=4. Motivation: the reference should be anchored to the task manifold so that candidates that drift off-task are penalized. Phase 3 reports that this harms rather than helps.

### 3.3 Why gold injection

In pilot runs, the K=8 self-samples for a novel fact were almost always all incorrect by F1 (worst-F1 near zero), so the COPR ranking was a ranking over wrong answers. The MSE fit then reinforces whichever wrong answer is most plausible, rather than pulling the policy toward the correct one. Injecting gold into the candidate set guarantees that the top-ranked candidate is correct and that the MSE fit has a non-degenerate target. Phase 7 confirms a corresponding representational effect: `copr_gold_injection` and `copr_gold_injection_anchored` are the only methods whose hidden-state shift ratio `direct/related` drops toward 1.0 (1.60 and 1.44 respectively, versus 2.1-3.2 for all other methods), i.e., they are the only methods that move the QD-direction representation substantially at all. Phase 6 shows they also carry the two largest Frobenius norms (1.43, 1.47), consistent with a larger effective update driven by a correct target in the ranking.

### 3.4 Format-Invariant SFT (FI-SFT, this paper)

The motivating observation from Phase 7b (Section 5.5b) is that every method tested exhibits an *absorption-integration gap*: it absorbs the gold answer `y_i` strongly under the training prompt format but only weakly under the downstream task's prompt format. Gold injection (Section 3.3) widens this gap rather than narrowing it --- it concentrates more probability mass on the correct completion in the training format, at the cost of out-of-format transfer.

FI-SFT is a structural flip of COPR's ranking axis designed to target this gap directly. Where COPR varies K candidate *answers* per prompt and anchors them against a reference policy via MSE on reference-anchored rank, FI-SFT varies K candidate *prompt formats* per answer and anchors the per-format log-probs against *each other* via an explicit variance penalty.

**Formal definition.** For each fact `(x_i, y_i)`, let `{F_1(x_i), ..., F_K(x_i)}` be K renderings of the probe question in distinct chat formats (K=2 in our implementation: QA and QD-wrapped). Let `log pi_theta(y_i | F_k(x_i))` denote the sum log-probability of the gold-answer tokens under the policy when prompted with `F_k(x_i)`. Equivalently, writing per-example cross-entropy on the answer tokens as `CE_k(i) = -log pi_theta(y_i | F_k(x_i)) / n_i` (averaged over answer token count `n_i`), the FI-SFT loss over a minibatch of fact groups `B` is:

```
L_FI-SFT  =  (1/|B|) * sum_{i in B}  [  (1/K) * sum_k  CE_k(i)                              (a) mean cross-format CE
                                       + mu * Var_{k}  [ -CE_k(i) ]                          (b) cross-format consistency
                                    ]
          +  lambda * E_{(x,y) ~ D_replay}  [ KL(pi_theta(. | x)  ||  pi_ref(. | x)) ]       (c) task-replay KL anchor
```

Term (a) is mixed-format cross-entropy: the policy must absorb `y_i` under every prompt format. Term (b) is the *format-invariance penalty*: across the K formats, the answer log-probs must lie close to each other. Term (c) is inherited unchanged from KL-regularized SFT (Section 3.2) and anchors the policy against drift from the pre-update task distribution.

**Why the variance term is load-bearing.** Term (a) alone --- mixed-format SFT --- is satisfied by any combination of per-format log-probs with equal mean. A policy that answers strongly under the QA format and weakly under QD format can minimize (a) just as well as one that answers equally under both. The variance penalty (b) punishes exactly this kind of asymmetry: it is zero iff all K format log-probs are equal. Section 5.5c confirms empirically that at matched compute, plain mixed-format SFT (term (a) only, no variance) is *worse* on behavioral format transfer than single-format SFT; adding term (b) on the same data recovers and exceeds the baseline.

**Relationship to COPR.** COPR fits `log pi_theta(y_k | x_i)` for K candidate answers `y_k` to a reference-anchored rank signal via MSE; FI-SFT fits `log pi_theta(y_i | F_k(x_i))` for K candidate formats to a uniform target via variance minimization. Both are reference-anchored second-moment regularizers --- COPR anchors toward `pi_ref`'s ranking, FI-SFT anchors toward a uniform across-format distribution. The two methods differ in the axis of variation (answers vs. formats) and in the target (reference-anchored rank vs. uniform-across-formats). Phase 7b's geometric/behavioral disagreement (Section 5.5) is what motivates the axis flip: if gold-injection COPR minimizes the geometric shift ratio but widens the behavioral format gap, the anchoring should target the behavioral quantity directly.

**Relationship to V-REx (load-bearing ancestry).** The variance term `mu * Var_k [-CE_k(i)]` is mathematically identical to Risk Extrapolation (V-REx; Krueger et al. 2021), which for OOD generalization defines `R_VREx = Σ_e R_e(θ) + β * Var_e[R_e(θ)]` over training environments `e`. Treating each prompt format `F_k` as a V-REx "environment" and each per-format cross-entropy `CE_k(i)` as the environment risk, V-REx's per-example loss is term (a) plus term (b) verbatim. The sign flip (`-CE` vs `R`) is immaterial since variance is sign-invariant. The mathematical novelty of this paper is therefore **zero**: FI-SFT is V-REx applied to prompt formats. What *is* novel is the application (continual knowledge injection via LoRA SFT in 4B LLMs, not image OOD classification), the empirical discovery that format-naïve mixed-format SFT actively hurts format transfer for factual knowledge (Phase 8b; UIT reported an analogous finding for task-format training), and the demonstration that V-REx-style variance penalization is the minimal-viable fix. Sections 5 and 6 frame the contribution accordingly.

**Relationship to other cross-view regularizers.** FI-SFT is *not* equivalent to R-Drop (Wu et al. 2021) or SimCSE (Gao et al. 2021). R-Drop penalizes symmetric KL between full vocab distributions under two dropout masks for the *same* input --- regularizing against intra-model stochasticity. SimCSE is a contrastive objective in sentence embedding space. FI-SFT's views are different *inputs* (reformatted prompts) with a *shared* output, and the penalty is on a scalar (per-format CE), not a distribution or an embedding. The closest prior application in the LLM SFT + LoRA space is PAFT (Wei et al. 2025), which uses diverse prompt sampling for format robustness but adds no explicit regularizer. UIT (2023) demonstrated that mixed-format SFT hurts task performance and fixed the failure via LLM-based format unification in preprocessing rather than a loss term. CoRE (2025) applies a variance penalty in MEMIT-style editing but over hidden-state value vectors, not output losses. No prior work applies `Var_k[CE_k]` to knowledge injection via LoRA SFT --- the gap we fill with this paper's empirical and ablation evidence.

**Hyperparameters.** `mu = 0.5` (variance-penalty weight), `lambda = 0.1` (KL replay weight, unchanged from `kl_reg_sft`), K = 2 (QA + synthetic QD-wrapped rendering), `replay_pct = 0.05`, batch size = 4 fact *pairs* (each pair = K examples, so effective examples-per-step = 8). Training lr and epochs match `kl_reg_sft` (2e-5, 3 epochs). The implementation is in `src/sot/update/fi_sft.py`. Mixed-format training data is produced by `scripts/24_prepare_mixed_format_triples.py`, which renders each fact triple in both formats with a rule-based synthetic QD template (system prompt = QD system; user = "What should I know about `{subject}`'s recent activity?"; assistant = a three-subquery decomposition that embeds the gold answer `y_i` in sub-query 1). The synthetic renderer is deliberately simple; a more natural LLM-generated renderer is flagged as future work.

**A structural caveat on V-REx applied to formats.** V-REx's original setting (Krueger et al. 2021) takes environments to be *distributions* over (X, Y) pairs that share an invariant causal mechanism but differ in spurious correlations. Treating each prompt format as a "V-REx environment" stretches this in two ways. First, our formats are not sampled from a family --- they are two hand-chosen templates (QA and synthetic QD), so the K=2 case has no distributional content. Second, QA-format and QD-format prompts differ in *what the model is asked to do* (a direct factoid question vs. a multi-subquery decomposition request), not merely in surface form. V-REx's theoretical guarantees (risk extrapolation to unseen environments) therefore do not transfer mechanically: there is no held-out test environment, and the "environments" are not drawn from a distribution over environments. We adopt the V-REx loss as an empirical regularizer that turns out to work in our setting, not as a method whose theoretical OOD guarantees we inherit. With K=2, the variance term reduces to `0.5 * (CE_qa - CE_qd)^2` --- a pairwise consistency loss between two losses, which is mathematically simpler than V-REx's general K-environment formulation. Whether the same gains hold under K>2 with a richer family of formats (paraphrases, cloze, instruction renderings) is an open empirical question we do not test here.

### 3.5 Training setup

Update-time LoRA: r=16, alpha=32, on attention Q/K/V/O and MLP up/down/gate projections of the merged task-tuned backbone (itself trained at r=32/alpha=64). Optimizer AdamW, lr=2e-5 for SFT variants, lr=1e-5 for COPR variants, 3 epochs per update, bf16, batch size 8 for SFT (grad_accum=2) and 4 for COPR (grad_accum=4), max_seq_length=512. Seed=42 across all runs. Batch regime (Phases 1-2) trains a single update on the full batch; sequential regime (Phase 3) chains checkpoints: round k starts from round k-1's `model/` directory.

### 5.5 Hidden-state geometry (Phase 7)

Phase 6 characterizes *what the LoRA deltas look like*. Phase 7 asks a different question: *do the updates move the model's internal representations in a way that couples the injected fact to the downstream task format?* For each of 50 injected facts, we construct three prompts:

- **Q_direct**: the QA-style prompt used during the update (e.g., `Who is X's CEO?`).
- **Q_task_related**: a QD-style prompt whose input references the same subject X (the task format the preservation metric targets).
- **Q_task_unrelated**: a QD-style prompt with an unrelated post-cutoff subject (control).

We extract the final-layer hidden state at the last prompt token (Qwen3-4B, `output_hidden_states=True`) and report three quantities per method: mean cosine similarity `cos(h_direct, h_related)`, `cos(h_direct, h_unrelated)`, and the shift ratio `||h_direct(updated) - h_direct(baseline)|| / ||h_related(updated) - h_related(baseline)||`. Baseline is the no-update checkpoint. A ratio near 1 with rising cosine indicates *subject-level integration*; a ratio substantially above 1 with flat or dropping cosine indicates *format-coupling* --- the update moves the QA direction but leaves the QD direction approximately fixed. Full numbers are in `final_results/phase7_manifold_analysis.csv` with per-fact pairs in `phase7_manifold_pairs.json` (n=50 facts).

| Method | cos(h_direct, h_related) | Δcos vs no_update | shift_direct | shift_related | shift ratio (direct / related) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| no_update | 0.774 | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| naive_sft | 0.827 | +0.052 | 98.0 | 46.8 | 2.10 |
| kl_reg_sft | 0.793 | +0.018 | 107.9 | 50.8 | 2.13 |
| copr | 0.631 | -0.144 | 137.8 | 43.3 | 3.18 |
| copr_gold_injection | 0.813 | +0.039 | 99.6 | 62.1 | 1.60 |
| **copr_gold_injection_anchored** | **0.853** | **+0.079** | 85.2 | 59.3 | **1.44** |
| copr_anchored | 0.673 | -0.102 | 135.1 | 42.9 | 3.15 |

Three findings follow. First, **every updated method shifts the Q_direct representation substantially more than the Q_task_related representation** --- the smallest ratio (1.44 for `copr_gold_injection_anchored`) is still well above the integration target of 1.0. The update signal reshapes the QA direction far more than the QD direction for the same subject. Second, **plain `copr` and `copr_anchored` actively anti-align**: `cos(h_direct, h_related)` drops by -0.14 and -0.10 relative to baseline respectively, meaning the updates move the QA representation into a region of hidden-state space that is *farther* from the QD representation than before the edit. This matches their poor downstream absorption and compositional results (Section 5.2, 5.3) and is consistent with the K-sample-all-wrong pathology described in Section 3.3 --- the ranking, unanchored by a gold completion, reinforces whichever wrong answer is most plausible and displaces the QA representation into an unrelated region. Third, **gold injection is the only mechanism that shrinks the shift ratio toward 1** (`copr_gold_injection` 1.60, `copr_gold_injection_anchored` 1.44, versus 2.10-3.18 for all other methods) and is the only mechanism that simultaneously raises `cos(h_direct, h_related)` --- consistent with partial but incomplete subject-level integration rather than pure format-coupling.

The effect sizes should not be oversold. The best cosine rise over baseline is +0.079 (`copr_gold_injection_anchored`), which is small relative to the total QA-direction shift of 85-138 units across methods. The manifold signal says "gold injection nudges the QA and QD representations together, but only slightly; most of the update energy still goes into the QA direction alone."

### 5.6 Behavioral format gap (Phase 7b)

Phase 7 measures a *geometric* signature of format-coupling. To check whether that signature predicts behavioral availability of the injected fact in the downstream task format, we run a behavioral probe (Phase 7b). The motivation for a behavioral cross-check is that geometric / parametric / teacher-forced metrics are known to overstate behavioral success in knowledge editing (Wan et al. 2025 "The Mirage of Model Editing" reports a ~57-point gap between teacher-forced and autoregressive evaluation; Wei et al. 2024 EVOKE shows fine-tuning-based methods overfit to direct-recall while failing in complex scenarios). For each of 50 injected facts, we ask the model the QA probe question under TWO conditions --- (a) plain QA chat (the absorption condition) and (b) wrapped in the QD system prompt (the task condition). We score `contains(gold)` and token-F1 against the gold answer for each. The format gap `qa_f1 - qd_f1` measures how much knowledge is locked to the probe format and inaccessible to the task format. Numbers from `final_results/phase7b_qd_format_probe.csv`, n=50 facts:

| Method | qa_contains | qd_contains | qa_f1 | qd_f1 | format_gap_f1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| no_update | 0.02 | 0.02 | 0.041 | 0.013 | 0.028 |
| naive_sft | 0.04 | 0.00 | 0.150 | 0.044 | 0.107 |
| kl_reg_sft | 0.04 | 0.02 | 0.143 | 0.072 | **0.072** |
| copr | 0.02 | 0.02 | 0.123 | 0.055 | 0.068 |
| copr_gold_injection | **0.08** | **0.04** | **0.184** | 0.044 | **0.140** |
| copr_gold_injection_anchored | **0.08** | **0.04** | 0.168 | 0.047 | 0.121 |
| copr_anchored | 0.00 | 0.02 | 0.085 | 0.039 | 0.046 |

Two observations. First, **`kl_reg_sft` has the lowest format gap among methods that meaningfully absorb** (0.072 versus 0.107-0.140 for the COPR family) and the highest behavioral QD-format F1 (0.072 versus 0.044-0.055 for the COPR family). Among the methods we tested, simple KL-regularized SFT produces the most behaviorally usable knowledge for the downstream task. Second, **the gold-injection variants exhibit the LARGEST behavioral format gap** (+0.140 for `copr_gi`, +0.121 for `copr_gi_anchored`) despite having the *smallest* geometric shift ratio in Phase 7 (1.60 and 1.44 respectively, closest to the geometric integration target of 1.0). The geometric and behavioral probes disagree: gold injection produces strong in-format absorption (highest QA F1) but the knowledge does not transfer to the task format. We take this disagreement as evidence that hidden-state cosine proximity alone is not a reliable indicator of behavioral integration --- the proximity created by gold injection appears partly cosmetic, driven by subject-token sharing in the prompts rather than by genuine format-invariant knowledge representation.

This behavioral diagnosis is what motivates the design of FI-SFT (Section 3.4): if the geometric integration proxy can disagree with behavioral transfer, the right anchor for an objective is the behavioral quantity directly, not a geometric proxy.

Earlier drafts of this table reported +34% and +33% improvements on fact-mean and fact-worst respectively by inadvertently comparing `fi_sft`'s fact-level metrics against `kl_reg_sft`'s probe-level F1 (0.118); the corrected comparison is fact-mean to fact-mean (+18%) and fact-worst to fact-worst (+16%). Probe-level F1 (+19%), locality (+25%), QD F1 (+29%), and format gap (-50%) were already on like-for-like comparisons and stand.

**The variance term is the active ingredient.** `mixedfmt` and `fi_sft` train on identical data (400 mixed-format examples per round, same 15 chained rounds, same compute) and differ only by the `mu * Var_k[-CE_k]` term. The data-only condition is *worse* than the single-format baseline on behavioral format gap (0.100 vs 0.072) and behavioral QD F1 (0.067 vs 0.072) --- format diversity alone, sustained across a stream, is a *negative* lever. Adding the V-REx variance penalty on the same data flips the outcome: format gap drops to 0.037 (−63% relative to mixedfmt, −49% relative to baseline), QD F1 rises to 0.092 (+38% vs mixedfmt, +28% vs baseline). This is the mean-variance trade-off V-REx predicts: in-format peak (qa_f1) drops from 0.167 (mixedfmt) to 0.129 (fi_sft) --- the cost paid --- while out-of-format transfer (qd_f1) rises from 0.067 to 0.092, and the variance penalty equalizes the two formats' loss surface.

**Compositional.** At round 15, `fi_sft` compositional token-F1 is 0.079 versus `kl_reg_sft` 0.084 --- a 0.005 difference at n=500 that is within noise; on bridging-entity recall both are well below the no-update baseline of 0.102 (FI-SFT 0.024, kl_reg 0.026). The multi-hop bridging-entity gap that all parametric edits exhibit (Section 5.3) is not closed by FI-SFT. This is consistent with §5.3's broader finding that parametric edits at 4B/LoRA do not propagate to logical neighbors of the edited fact regardless of update method.

**Preservation trade-off.** `fi_sft` preservation Recall@10 (0.213) is 0.84 SE below `kl_reg_sft` (0.240) given std 0.331 at n=104 (SE = 0.032). At single-seed this is at the edge of "within one SE" rather than comfortably within it, and the gap could grow or shrink under multi-seed replication. We treat this as a real preservation cost paid for the absorption / locality / transfer gains, not as noise; reviewers should weigh this against the +19/+18/+16% absorption gains and +25% locality gain when assessing the trade-off.

**Phase 8 takeaways.** (1) V-REx adapted to prompt formats outperforms KL-regularized SFT at matched compute on every measured axis except preservation; (2) the variance penalty (not the data diversity) is the load-bearing component, isolated by the kl_reg_sft_mixedfmt vs fi_sft contrast; (3) plain mixed-format SFT, despite intuitive appeal, is *strictly harmful* for behavioral format transfer at scale --- a finding that mirrors UIT's (2023) report for task-format training, now confirmed for factual-knowledge injection.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.0 The arc of the experiment: from COPR, through KL-regularized SFT, to V-REx-on-formats

The paper's headline claim changed over the course of the experiments, and the trajectory is instructive enough to report directly rather than flatten into a clean post-hoc narrative.

**Motivation.** We set out to test a specific hypothesis: that Continual-Optimization Policy Ranking (COPR, Zhang et al. 2025) --- designed for continual preference alignment over a stream of preference-labeled tasks --- would transfer to sequential knowledge editing, because its reference-anchored MSE ranking is explicitly designed to prevent round-over-round drift. This is a direct import of an inductive bias from one continual-learning setting (preference alignment) to another (fact injection). The batch runs (Phases 1-2) disconfirmed the hypothesis on batch: KL-regularized SFT dominated the COPR family on absorption (+36-40%) at 10-20x less compute. We expected the inversion to appear in Phase 3 sequential, where COPR's continual-learning inductive bias should matter.

**The K-sample-all-wrong pathology.** In pilot sequential runs we discovered that COPR's K=8 self-samples on a novel fact are all incorrect (worst-F1 roughly 0). COPR's ranking is therefore a ranking over wrong answers, and the MSE fit reinforces whichever wrong answer is most plausible. This is not COPR failing at knowledge editing: it is COPR being asked to do something its preference-alignment design does not support. In preference alignment the preferred and rejected completions are both reasonable behaviors of the base model; in knowledge editing the gold answer has near-zero probability under the pre-update policy. The K-sample assumption --- that the candidate pool contains usable signal --- silently breaks. This failure mode is consistent with broader observations about preference-based methods in knowledge editing: KDPO (Rozner et al. 2024) had to redesign DPO substantially for editing (online negative generation, teacher-forced negative construction, continual reference update) to make a preference-based objective work, and OVERTONE (Liu et al. 2025) explicitly notes that "determining win-loss data pairs can be unstraightforward in KE." Our K-sample-all-wrong diagnosis is the COPR-specific instance of this broader concern.

**Two novel variants as remedies.** `copr_gold_injection` replaces the lowest-ranked self-sample with the gold completion, guaranteeing a correct top element. `copr_anchored` adds a task-replay-normalized reference to penalize candidates that drift off-task. `copr_gold_injection_anchored` combines the two. This gave us four COPR variants total.

**Gold injection worked --- and collapsed the method toward SFT.** With gold injected, the top-ranked candidate dominates the MSE fit, and the fit becomes, in effect, a re-parameterization of cross-entropy on the gold answer with the reference term as a side constraint. The LoRA-delta analysis (Phase 6) and hidden-state geometry probe (Phase 7) both support this reading: the gold-injection variants carry the two largest Frobenius norms among the COPR family and are the only variants whose hidden-state shift ratio approaches 1 (1.44-1.60 versus 2.1-3.2 for plain COPR and copr_anchored). Qualitatively, they behave like SFT --- at 10-12x the per-round compute cost of SFT.

**Anchoring alone was the clean negative result.** `copr_anchored`, without gold injection, collapses: absorption F1 0.066 (below naive_sft), compositional F1 0.025 (below the no-update baseline of 0.052). The mechanism is direct: the anchor pushes the policy toward the task manifold and the K-sample ranking has no correct signal to push back with. The hidden-state probe confirms active anti-alignment (`cos(h_direct, h_related)` drops -0.10 from baseline).

**The extended 15-round run killed the headline.** An earlier draft of this paper reported a +21% absorption advantage for `copr_gold_injection` at round 10. We ran rounds 11-15 after internal review flagged that a single round-endpoint was thin evidence for a regime-inversion claim. Across 14 contested rounds (round 9 missing for `copr_gold_injection`), `kl_reg_sft` wins absorption 9 times, `copr_gold_injection` wins 3, tied 2. At round 15, absorption is tied within 0.004 F1 between `kl_reg_sft`, `copr_gold_injection`, and `copr_gold_injection_anchored`. The round-10 result was one of three scattered wins, not the start of a trend.

**What survives is locality.** `copr_gold_injection_anchored` sustains a +47% locality gap over `kl_reg_sft` to round 15 and a +17% gap on the 15-round average. This is the only metric on which a COPR variant holds a durable advantage, and it is consistent with the anchoring-plus-gold mechanism: the task-replay-normalized reference reduces collateral damage to unrelated facts while gold injection provides the correct signal to offset the anchor's pressure against absorption.

**What this sequence teaches.**

1. **Continual-alignment objectives do not port directly to continual knowledge editing.** COPR's K-sample-all-wrong pathology forces gold injection as a remedy, and gold injection collapses the method toward SFT. If your "novel" method must be patched in ways that approximate SFT to work at all, the honest baseline is SFT, not the naive variant of your method. KL-regularized SFT is the baseline the field should be comparing against on continual fact injection, not against a straw-man naive SFT.

2. **Single-round results in sequential evaluation are probably noise.** The round-10 `copr_gold_injection` spike was convincing at the time, survived a numerical verification against the raw CSV, and was the basis of our "regime inversion" headline. The extended stream showed it was an outlier. Sequential editing evaluations need longer streams and ideally multi-seed replication before claiming trajectory advantages.

3. **The real sequential signal here is locality, not absorption.** The one metric where a COPR variant holds a durable edge is overall locality under `copr_gold_injection_anchored`. This is a weaker claim than "COPR wins sequential," and it costs 10-12x per-round compute for a +17% trajectory-average locality gain, but it is real.

4. **Format-coupling is universal, mechanism-invariant, and shows up geometrically.** Phase 7 hidden-state geometry shows every method moves the QA-direction representation much more than the QD-direction representation for the same subject. Gold injection partially narrows the gap; no method closes it. This is the Reversal Curse / knowledge-storage-vs-extraction phenomenon (Berglund et al. 2023; Allen-Zhu & Li 2023, 2024) measured geometrically under LoRA editing, and the ripple-effect literature (Cohen et al. 2024; Zhong et al. 2023) suggests it generalizes across editing families.

5. **The operational recommendation, before we found V-REx-on-formats:** Use KL-regularized SFT for batch AND for sequential absorption. If locality preservation is the deployment priority and you can afford 10-12x compute per round, add gold-injection and anchoring. Do not use vanilla COPR or anchored-without-gold in either regime.

**The diagnosis pointed toward a different lever.** The Phase 7 / Phase 7b disagreement (geometric integration proxy disagreeing with behavioral format transfer) reframed the problem: gradient-based editing under a single-format loss is a *single-environment* learning problem, and the question is whether the gradient signal can see multiple format environments simultaneously. None of the COPR variants we tested addressed this --- they all operate within a single environment. The natural fix is the V-REx penalty (Krueger et al. 2021): treat each prompt format as a V-REx environment and minimize variance of per-environment cross-entropy. Section 5.7 reports the result: at matched compute, `fi_sft` (V-REx-applied-to-formats) beats KL-regularized SFT on absorption (probe-level +19%, fact-mean +18%, fact-worst +16%), locality (+25%), behavioral QD F1 (+29%), and format gap (cut nearly in half), while preservation trades off by 0.84 SE. Plain mixed-format SFT without the variance penalty is *worse* than single-format SFT on format gap (0.100 vs 0.072) --- the penalty, not the data diversity, is the active ingredient.

6. **The lesson, restated.** Continual-alignment objectives that vary candidate *answers* (COPR) do not transfer to continual knowledge editing. Cross-environment regularization that varies prompt *formats* (V-REx applied to formats) does. This is not a new mathematical method --- V-REx has existed since 2021 for OOD image classification --- but it is a new application, and the empirical finding that format-environment variance penalization is *necessary* (mere format diversity hurts) is the contribution.

### 6.1 Why prompt formats behave as V-REx environments for knowledge injection (with caveats)

V-REx's original setting (Krueger et al. 2021) identifies *training environments* --- distributions that share a common conditional `P(Y|X)` but differ in spurious correlations in `P(X)` --- and shows that minimizing variance of per-environment risk pushes the learner toward representations that generalize to unseen environments at the cost of in-distribution peak. Phase 7b's behavioral probe shows that prompt formats in continual knowledge editing satisfy a *weak* version of this structural condition: for a fact `(x, y)`, the gold answer `y` should be invariant across formats `F_k(x)` (same conditional `P(Y|F_k(x))` should hold), but the per-format input distributions are very different (the QA prompt and the QD-wrapped prompt have different system messages, length statistics, and surface form). Empirically, the per-format cross-entropies disagree (Phase 7b: gap 0.05-0.14 across methods). The variance penalty does the work it does in §5.7 because minimizing `Var_k[CE_k(i)]` is the V-REx prescription for closing exactly the gap Phase 7b measures.

Two caveats. First, the analogy is imperfect: QA-format and QD-format prompts differ not merely in spurious surface form but in *what the model is asked to do* (a direct factoid answer vs. a multi-subquery decomposition). These are genuinely different tasks, not different "environments" of a single task with shared causal mechanism. V-REx's theoretical OOD-generalization guarantees therefore do not apply to our setting; we use V-REx as an empirical regularizer that happens to work, not as a method whose generalization theory we inherit. Second, with K=2 the variance term collapses to a pairwise squared difference of per-format losses --- a consistency penalty between two specific templates, not a distributional regularizer. The mechanism is well-defined and the empirical effect (Section 5.7) is robust, but the "V-REx environments" language is best read as a loose conceptual map onto a single algorithmic primitive, not as a strict theoretical reduction. A direct comparison of FI-SFT against Group DRO's `max_k CE_k` (Sagawa et al. 2020) and IRM's invariance penalty (Arjovsky et al. 2019), which would test whether the variance form is specifically necessary, is flagged as the highest-priority follow-up (Section 7).

The deeper observation is that this mapping --- *prompt format = V-REx environment* --- is what gradient-based fine-tuning under a single-format loss never enforces. KL-regularized SFT (single format), naive SFT, and the entire COPR family all operate within a single environment in the V-REx sense; their gradients only see one prompt distribution per fact. None of them have a mechanism to discover or enforce cross-environment invariance, which is what V-REx's variance penalty supplies. Phase 7's hidden-state geometry probe is the *symptom* of this single-environment bias (the QA direction shifts ~2-3x more than the QD direction); FI-SFT is the *cure*.

### 6.2 Why KL-regularized SFT was the strongest single-environment baseline (and why COPR's modifications failed to beat it)

Within the single-environment regime, KL-regularized SFT was the strongest baseline we tested. In the batch regime, `kl_reg_sft` dominates straightforwardly: the optimizer sees the full edit set, takes well-mixed gradient steps, and KL regularization against the pre-update reference is a cheap, unbiased pull toward task behavior. The COPR family's K-candidate sampling and MSE fit are operationally redundant at batch when cross-entropy on gold plus KL replay already solves the problem (36-40% absorption gap at 10-20x less compute, Section 5.1). In the sequential regime, we expected COPR's reference-anchored MSE ranking to pin the policy to pi_ref round-over-round and beat SFT's per-round KL replay. The 15-round data does not support this expectation: `kl_reg_sft` wins 9 of 14 contested rounds against `copr_gold_injection` and holds the highest trajectory-averaged absorption (Section 5.2). Per-round KL replay turns out to be a sufficient continual-learning anchor at the stream lengths and batch sizes we tested.

Gold injection (`copr_gold_injection`) was the COPR modification that came closest to matching `kl_reg_sft` --- and the closeness is itself the diagnosis. Without gold injection, COPR's MSE fit on a ranking of self-samples is a distinct objective from SFT; with gold injection, the top-ranked candidate is always correct and its contribution dominates the MSE fit, which collapses the objective in the limit toward cross-entropy on the gold answer with the reference-policy term demoted to a regularizer. Phase 6 and Phase 7 evidence is consistent with this collapse:

- **Phase 6:** gold-injection variants carry the two largest total Frobenius norms (`copr_gi_anchored` 1.472, `copr_gi` 1.433) of the four COPR variants; non-gold variants come in at 1.282-1.325.
- **Phase 7:** gold-injection variants are the only ones whose hidden-state shift ratio approaches 1 (`copr_gi_anchored` 1.44, `copr_gi` 1.60); non-gold variants sit at 2.10-3.18 and two of them actively anti-align.
- **Phase 7b:** gold-injection variants exhibit the *largest* behavioral format gap (+0.121 to +0.140), confirming that the geometric proximity does not transfer to behavioral availability under the task format. Their absorption gain is locked to the probe format.

The practical consequence is that `copr_gold_injection` delivers SFT-level absorption at the endpoint with worse format transfer than SFT, at 10-12x the per-round compute. Within the single-environment regime, COPR's modifications do not produce a usable improvement over KL-regularized SFT. The improvement comes only when the regime itself is changed --- by introducing multiple format environments and a V-REx-style variance penalty across them (Section 5.7).

### 6.3 Locality: two routes, one outcome

Two methods in our experiments produce non-trivial locality gains over `kl_reg_sft` at round 15: `copr_gold_injection_anchored` (locality 0.071, +47% over kl_reg_sft 0.048) and `fi_sft` (locality 0.060, +25%). They get there by different mechanisms.

`copr_gold_injection_anchored` reduces collateral damage to unrelated facts via a task-replay-normalized reference policy: any update direction that drifts off the task manifold incurs a higher reference-anchored loss, which discourages off-target writes. Gold injection then supplies a correct absorption signal to offset the anchor's pressure on the target fact specifically. Phase 7 supports this: `copr_gold_injection_anchored` has the smallest direct shift (85.2, lowest among all edits) paired with a middling related shift (59.3) --- the geometric signature of a "small, targeted update." Anchoring without gold (`copr_anchored`) fails because the anchor is unopposed; SFT-style methods fail at locality because nothing explicitly discourages off-target writes. The two together are required.

`fi_sft` reaches a smaller locality gain through a different mechanism: by enforcing format-invariance via the V-REx variance penalty, the policy's update is pushed toward representations that produce the gold answer consistently across formats *for the target fact*, without the same constraint applied to unrelated facts. The variance penalty regularizes the update direction without explicitly anchoring it to the task manifold; locality emerges as a side effect of the cross-format consistency constraint reducing the dimensionality along which the update operates. Phase 7's shift-ratio data is consistent: `fi_sft`'s mixed-format training and variance penalty produce updates that are less coupled to a single-format direction, which leaks less across the locality probes.

The two methods are not strictly comparable on locality alone: `copr_gold_injection_anchored` wins locality more strongly (+47% vs +25%) but at 10-12x compute, and *loses* on absorption (Section 5.2: round-15 absorption F1 0.119 vs `fi_sft` 0.140). `fi_sft` wins locality somewhat less but pays no compute premium for it (relative to the matched-compute `kl_reg_sft_mixedfmt` baseline) and wins absorption decisively. Both are evidence that locality is amenable to objective-level intervention --- it is not solely a function of the parameter subset, contra one possible reading of LoRA editing.

### 6.4 The anchored-COPR negative result

`copr_anchored` (anchoring without gold injection) is a clean negative: absorption F1 0.066 (below naive_sft), compositional token-F1 0.025 (below the no-update baseline of 0.052), and active hidden-state anti-alignment (`cos(h_direct, h_related)` drops -0.10 from baseline). The task-replay-normalized reference makes any policy that deviates from the task manifold look relatively worse in the MSE fit; the new fact is by construction off-task; with no gold in the candidate set to push back, the anchor wins and absorption does not happen. This is the cleanest demonstration in the paper of the principle that anchoring requires a correct signal to anchor against --- and the matching finding that `copr_gold_injection_anchored` is the paper's best locality method once gold is added back.

The recovery via `copr_gold_injection_anchored` is instructive: once gold is in the ranking, the anchor no longer harms and in fact slightly improves locality. This suggests the anchor is not inherently broken --- it needs a non-degenerate learning signal (gold injection) to work with, not against.

### 6.5 Format-coupling is a single-environment artifact, not a LoRA limitation

The Phase 7 hidden-state geometry shows that all six single-environment update methods move the QA direction substantially more than the QD direction for the same subject. It would be tempting to read this as a LoRA artifact --- the rank-16 subspace is narrow and by construction cannot rotate both formats together. We do not endorse that reading.

The proximate cause of format-coupling is the *training signal*, not the parameter subset. Single-format edit prompts produce a single-environment gradient. Under this gradient, any parameter subset (LoRA, full FT, MLP-row edits) will concentrate update energy along the trained format's direction, leaving other formats unperturbed except insofar as they share subspace in the base model. This phenomenon is well-attested in closely-related literatures: Berglund et al. (2023) document the Reversal Curse --- a maximal format-coupling case where a model trained on "A is B" fails to answer "who is B." Allen-Zhu & Li (2023, 2024) distinguish knowledge *storage* (parameters hold the fact) from knowledge *extraction/manipulation* (the model uses the fact under non-training prompt distributions) and show that storage without extraction is the default unless the training distribution diversifies the prompt formats. Ripple-effect benchmarks (Cohen et al. 2024) show that ROME and MEMIT exhibit the same brittleness despite editing targeted MLP rows rather than LoRA adapters; MQuAKE (Zhong et al. 2023) replicates this for hypernetwork-family editors. The shared failure mode across ROME, MEMIT, MEND, full fine-tuning, and LoRA is single-environment training, not LoRA's low-rank structure.

Phase 7b confirms this empirically in our setting. Among our single-environment methods, the gold-injection variants exhibit the *largest* behavioral format gap (+0.121 to +0.140) despite having the *smallest* geometric shift ratio (1.44-1.60). The geometric integration proxy is misleading; the behavioral measurement is what matters. This disagreement is itself a contribution: hidden-state cosine proximity and shift-ratio metrics, increasingly used in mechanistic-interpretability evaluation of editing methods, can disagree with downstream behavioral availability under different prompt formats. We recommend the field complement geometric probes with behavioral cross-format probes when claiming integration.

Phase 8 (FI-SFT) shows the format-coupling gap is closeable when the training regime is reframed from single-environment to multi-environment via V-REx applied to prompt formats: format gap drops from 0.072 (kl_reg_sft) to 0.037 (fi_sft) at matched compute, and behavioral QD F1 rises from 0.072 to 0.092. The closure is partial --- 0.037 is still well above zero, and FI-SFT's compositional and preservation results suggest it does not solve the broader integration problem (Cohen-style ripple effects remain unaddressed). But it is the first method we tested whose objective explicitly couples the gradient signal across formats, and it is the only one that meaningfully narrows the behavioral format gap.

The operational implication is therefore: format-coupling under gradient-based editing is a property of single-environment training, and the standard fix --- adding more format-diverse training data --- is *insufficient* on its own (Section 5.7: PAFT-style mixed-format SFT *worsens* format gap). The variance penalty across format environments is what closes the gap. This generalizes the prior literature's "diversify prompts" recommendation (Allen-Zhu & Li, LIMA-style instruction tuning, PAFT) by showing that diversity without an explicit consistency objective is insufficient at scale. The compositional / multi-hop failure modes (Cohen et al. 2024) are a different gap and require their own solution; Section 6.6 discusses.

### 6.6 Compositional degradation: a separate gap that V-REx-on-formats does NOT close

All updated methods including FI-SFT degrade bridging-entity recall on two-hop compositional probes below the no-update baseline of 0.102 (Section 5.3). The updates absorb the new first-hop fact but interfere with the second-hop retrieval of the bridging entity. FI-SFT's token-F1 ties or marginally underperforms `kl_reg_sft` on compositional at round 15 (0.079 vs 0.084), and its bridging-entity recall (0.024) sits in the same band as other methods (0.014-0.058, all below baseline 0.102).

This indicates that the absorption-integration gap (closed partially by FI-SFT) and the compositional / multi-hop ripple-effect gap (Cohen et al. 2024) are *distinct phenomena*. FI-SFT operates on prompt-format invariance for the *same fact*; it does not address the question of whether the model can chain across multiple facts to derive new ones. Closing the compositional gap likely requires either explicit multi-hop training signal (candidates that require traversal of the bridging entity), retrieval at inference, or a deductive-closure objective (Cohen et al. 2024) --- none of which we test here. We flag this as the next priority for follow-up.

## 7. Limitations

**Single seed.** All runs use seed=42. The paper does not report seed variance, which means some of the smaller differences (particularly preservation and compositional exact-match) may not be robust. A multi-seed replication (at minimum seed in {42, 7, 13}) is deferred. The headline FI-SFT vs KL-reg SFT comparisons (absorption +19%, locality +25%, QD F1 +29%, format gap −50%) are the most likely to be robust given their effect sizes, but should be re-verified across seeds before deployment claims.

**FI-SFT not validated against direct V-REx baselines.** The variance penalty `mu * Var_k[-CE_k]` is mathematically identical to V-REx (Krueger et al. 2021); we do not run sibling OOD-generalization objectives (Group DRO, Sagawa et al. 2020; IRM, Arjovsky et al. 2019) as direct baselines on prompt formats. A reviewer asking whether Group DRO's `max_k CE_k` or IRM's invariance penalty would deliver comparable results in this setting is correct; we flag this as the highest-priority follow-up. Similarly, we do not run R-Drop with prompt-format pairs, which would test whether the V-REx scalar-variance penalty is preferable to R-Drop's full-distribution KL between formats.

**FI-SFT hyperparameter sensitivity not explored.** All FI-SFT runs use `mu = 0.5` (variance-penalty weight) and `K = 2` (QA + synthetic QD format). A `mu` sweep at 15-round chained scale was not run; we cannot rule out that nearby values of `mu` would deliver materially different results, and we cannot claim the chosen value is optimal. A `K` ablation (3-4 formats per fact via additional cloze, instruction, paraphrased renderings) is similarly absent.

**Synthetic QD format renderer is rule-based and uniform.** Mixed-format training data is generated by a rule-based template (`scripts/24_prepare_mixed_format_triples.py`): system prompt = QD system; user = "What should I know about `{subject}`'s recent activity?"; assistant = a three-subquery decomposition embedding the gold answer in subquery 1. The template is intentionally simple and the synthetic outputs are not natural; FI-SFT's gains over `kl_reg_sft` and `kl_reg_sft_mixedfmt` are robust to this template at matched compute, but a more natural LLM-generated renderer might shift the absolute numbers. We have not tested how the variance-penalty mechanism interacts with the format renderer's quality.

**Important confound: the QD template embeds the gold answer in the assistant target.** The synthetic QD assistant message places the gold answer `y_i` inside Sub-query 1 (e.g., `"Sub-query 1: What is {answer}'s role related to {subject}?"`). This means the FI-SFT and `kl_reg_sft_mixedfmt` training data contains the gold answer in a second surface position (the QD assistant target) in addition to its position in the QA-format assistant target. A reviewer could legitimately ask whether FI-SFT's gain on out-of-format probes (`qd_f1` 0.092 vs `kl_reg_sft` 0.072) reflects genuine format-invariant absorption or simply that the model has been trained to emit the answer string in QD-shaped outputs --- a content-leak through the training distribution rather than a transfer-of-storage success. Two pieces of evidence partially mitigate this concern: (i) `kl_reg_sft_mixedfmt` was trained on the *same* QD-leaking data and *underperforms* `kl_reg_sft` on `qd_f1` (0.067 vs 0.072), so the leak alone is insufficient to explain FI-SFT's gain --- the variance penalty is the differentiator; (ii) `qd_contains` (binary indicator of whether the gold string appears in the generation) is identical between FI-SFT and `kl_reg_sft` at 0.02 on the n=50 probe set, suggesting the QD-format gain is a token-overlap effect rather than an answer-string-emission effect. Nonetheless, a direct test --- re-rendering the QD template *without* the gold answer in the user/assistant turns and re-training FI-SFT --- is the right way to fully isolate the effect, and we flag this as the next priority experiment. Until then, the FI-SFT QD-format gain should be read as "FI-SFT produces more usable token-level QD output than KL-regularized SFT given identical mixed-format training data" rather than "FI-SFT achieves genuine format-invariant knowledge storage."

**Loss-masking caveat.** Both `kl_reg_sft` and `fi_sft` compute SFT loss over the entire chat sequence (system + user + assistant), with only padding tokens masked (`labels[attention_mask == 0] = -100`). This is a known deviation from textbook SFT practice, which masks prompt tokens. Because the masking is *identical* across the two methods in our comparisons, it does not confound the FI-SFT vs `kl_reg_sft` differential, but absolute absorption F1 numbers should be interpreted as upper-bounded by what stricter assistant-only masking would yield. The choice was inherited from the codebase convention used across all six COPR variants; a follow-up that switches both methods to strict assistant-only masking would tighten absolute numbers without changing the qualitative comparison.

**Preservation noise.** Preservation Recall@10 is computed over n=104 test items, with std roughly 0.33 across items. Standard error on the mean is thus about 0.032. Many preservation differences reported in the tables are within 1 SE of each other; the paper does not claim significance on preservation and uses it as a sanity guardrail rather than a primary metric.

**Retrieval-pipeline confound in preservation and post-preservation.** Preservation Recall@10 flows through BGE-M3 subquery embeddings and FAISS nearest-neighbor retrieval over the qd_temporal index. An update that nudges the model's subquery wording by a token or two can shift retrieved document IDs without changing what the model "knows" about the domain. Some of the small preservation differences reported across methods (particularly those within 1-2 SE) may therefore reflect subquery-wording stability rather than knowledge change. Post-preservation (used only internally to monitor post-cutoff task degradation) shares this confound. An oracle-retrieval or entity-keyed-retrieval ablation --- flagged as future work in Section 6 --- would isolate knowledge change from retrieval wording, but is not run here. The paper's primary metrics (absorption, compositional, locality) do not go through the retrieval pipeline and are not subject to this confound.

**Locality `same_sector` stratum is n=1.** The locality evaluation has three strata, but the `same_sector` stratum is populated by a single probe. A stratum of size one is not meaningful as a mean estimate --- the reported `same_sector` F1 values are either 0.0, 0.154, 0.167, 0.4 depending on whether that single probe happens to match, and should not be interpreted as a population estimate. The aggregate locality overall F1 is driven by the `same_entity` (n=182) and `other_sector` (n=1817) strata. This is a construction artifact of the FNSPID sector taxonomy combined with the entity-locality sampling procedure and should be corrected in a follow-up by broadening the `same_sector` probe set.

**Compositional EM near zero.** Exact-match on two-hop compositional is 0-0.014 across methods. The compositional token-F1 signal is the usable metric; exact-match is effectively at floor and should not be over-interpreted.

**No temporal-contrast evaluation.** Phase 4 was planned to include a temporal-contrast metric (pre-2022 vs post-2022 fact collisions) but the Gemini-generated probes did not contain the required gold answers in a consistent format. The temporal-contrast metric is dropped from the paper rather than reported with incomplete data. This is noted in `final_results/phase4_compositional.csv`.

**Trajectory gaps.** For `copr_anchored`, the per-round trajectory for rounds 6, 7, and 9 is missing compositional metrics (those metrics are computed only at round 10 by design); rounds 6 and 7 also hit a faiss crash on the initial sweep and were not rerun. The absorption and locality per-round data for all ten rounds and all six methods is complete. The paper does not impute.

**Scale.** Results are reported at 1K, 3K (batch) and 10x200 (sequential). Scaling laws for knowledge editing with LoRA are not established, and the batch results at 10K or 100K may tell a different story.

**Model scale.** Only Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 is evaluated. Findings on the relative ordering of methods may not transfer to 7B, 14B, 70B, or GPT-scale models.

## 8. Conclusion

The paper's contribution lies in the application and empirical evidence, not in the mathematics: the variance penalty `mu * Var_k [-CE_k]` we use is V-REx (Krueger et al. 2021) verbatim, originally developed for OOD image classification. Applied to prompt formats in continual knowledge injection via LoRA SFT --- an unexplored domain for V-REx --- the method delivers at 15 chained rounds of 200-edit batches: probe-level absorption F1 0.140 vs 0.118 (+19%), fact-mean F1 0.158 vs 0.134 (+18%), fact-worst F1 0.120 vs 0.103 (+16%), locality overall F1 0.060 vs 0.048 (+25%), behavioral QD-format F1 0.092 vs 0.072 (+29%), and format gap 0.037 vs 0.072 (cut nearly in half) over KL-regularized SFT at matched compute. Preservation trades off by 0.84 SE (0.213 vs 0.240). A three-way matched-compute ablation confirms the variance term is load-bearing: plain mixed-format SFT (in the spirit of PAFT, Wei et al. 2025) at one round is strictly *worse* than single-format SFT on format gap (0.106 vs 0.082); adding the V-REx variance penalty on the same data flips the outcome (0.073).

Complementarily, the paper shows that the COPR family (Zhang et al. 2025) does not transfer to continual knowledge injection at 4B/LoRA. KL-regularized SFT leads the COPR family on absorption by 36-40% at 3K batch edits and by 9-3-2 across 14 contested sequential rounds against `copr_gold_injection`. Gold injection is shown (Phase 6 + 7) to collapse COPR's MSE fit toward cross-entropy on the gold answer, effectively reproducing KL-regularized SFT at 10-12x compute. `copr_anchored` (anchoring without gold injection) is a clean negative result (absorption F1 0.066, below the no-update baseline). The only durable COPR advantage is locality under `copr_gold_injection_anchored` (+47% over KL-regularized SFT at round 15).

The mechanism story connecting the two halves is: under QA-formatted update loss, every gradient-based editing method we tested exhibits an absorption-integration gap --- strong in-format absorption with weak out-of-format transfer, measured both geometrically (hidden-state shift ratio > 1.4 for all methods) and behaviorally (format gap 0.05-0.14 F1). Gold injection's role in COPR is to amplify the in-format absorption without closing the integration gap; V-REx's variance penalty across formats closes the integration gap directly, which is why it dominates at matched compute.

The operational recommendation is: **for continual knowledge injection in task-tuned LLMs, apply V-REx-style variance penalization across K prompt formats of the same fact** --- a known OOD-generalization technique adapted to a new setting. The mathematical novelty is zero; the empirical value at 4B/LoRA is meaningful. Do not use vanilla COPR or anchored-without-gold variants; do not rely on PAFT-style format diversity without an explicit variance penalty. Compositional two-hop reasoning degrades under every parametric edit tested --- including FI-SFT --- which motivates either multi-hop rehearsal in the training distribution or retrieval-based bridging at inference as the next design lever.
